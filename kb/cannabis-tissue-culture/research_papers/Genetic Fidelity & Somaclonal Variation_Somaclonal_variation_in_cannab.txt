</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>Anthocyanidins are bioactive compounds found mostly in colored plants and fruits. Consumption of anthocyanidin-rich foods has been shown to reduce the risk of diabetes. However, limited information is available regarding the inhibitory effect and interactions of anthocyanidins on α-glucosidase, the key enzyme that controls diabetes through degrading carbohydrate. Therefore, we used computational docking analysis to investigate the degree and type of inhibition by α-glucosidase, and the structural interactions of enzyme-selected anthocyanidins. The results suggested that anthocyanidins exhibit half maximal inhibitory concentration of 4∼55 μM; the strongest and weakest α-glucosidase inhibitors were delphinidin and malvidin, respectively. Indeed, delphinidin inhibits α-glucosidase in a mixed type, close to non-competitive manner with an inhibitory constant of 78 nM. Addition of a glycoside (glucoside or galactoside) at C3 on the C ring of delphinidin significantly decreased inhibitory activity, and addition of glycosides at C3 on the C ring and C5 on the A ring of delphinidin prevented all inhibitory activity. Molecular docking and free binding energy accurately confirmed the mode of inhibition determined by enzyme kinetics. These data will inform the use of alternative sources of anthocyanidins in functional foods and dietary supplements for prevention of diabetes. The results provide useful information for evaluating possible molecular models using anthocyanins/anthocyanidins as templates and α-glucosidase as the key enzyme in management of diabetes.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> anthocyanidins, anthocyanins, anti-α-glucosidase activity, enzyme kinetics, molecular docking</p></section></section><section id="sec1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">INTRODUCTION</h2>
<p>Type II diabetes mellitus (DM) is a metabolic disorder correlated with high blood glucose levels (hyperglycemia) due to insulin hormone resistance of β cells in the pancreas (<a href="#ref29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref29">Shrivastava et al., 2013</a>). Numerous treatment options for type II DM are available, including non-pharmacological therapy (e.g. appropriate diet, exercise, weight loss, and lifestyle modification) and pharmacological interventions. The latter focuses on inhibition of enzymes that control carbohydrate degradation, α-amylase, and α-glucosidase (<a href="#ref10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref10">Hui et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#ref20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref20">Nyenwe et al., 2011</a>). Inhibition of these enzymes retards carbohydrate digestion and glucose absorption, which, in turn, reduces the level of postprandial hyperglycemia following a meal, and decreases triglyceride synthesis in adipose tissue, liver, and intestinal walls (<a href="#ref33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref33">van de Laar, 2008</a>; <a href="#ref3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref3">de la Garza et al., 2013</a>). Several medicinal treatments have been proposed from a pharmacological perspective with an effective dose; however, synthetic drugs are expensive, and often have severe side effects with responses dependent on the individual. Therefore, bioactive compounds with potential antidiabetic properties from natural products have recently attracted increasing interest.</p>
<p>Bioactive compounds derived from plants are generally phenolics, and are defined as phenolic acids, coumarins, flavonoids, and tannins. Among the phenolics, flavonoids are effective anti-α-glucosidase agents with potentially higher half maximal inhibitory concentration (IC<sub>50</sub>) than phenolic acids, coumarins, and tannins (<a href="#ref31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref31">Tadera et al., 2006</a>; <a href="#ref17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref17">Mohamed Sham Shihabudeen et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#ref41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref41">Yin et al., 2014</a>; <a href="#ref44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref44">Zhu et al., 2014</a>). Many previous studies have suggested that consumption of foods containing high levels of flavonoids prevent diabetes through inhibiting α-glucosidase (<a href="#ref1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref1">Bahadoran et al., 2013</a>; <a href="#ref32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref32">Testa et al., 2016</a>). Therefore, flavonoid-rich foods have been predicted to represent a potential new method for management of diabetes with reduced adverse effects and increased economical effectiveness compared with commercial synthetic drugs. Interestingly, the inhibitory effectiveness of flavonoids against α-glucosidase was higher than for α-amylase, suggesting that the former enzyme may be a more practical target for controlling diabetes (<a href="#ref31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref31">Tadera et al., 2006</a>; <a href="#ref17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref17">Mohamed Sham Shihabudeen et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#ref41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref41">Yin et al., 2014</a>; <a href="#ref44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref44">Zhu et al., 2014</a>).</p>
<p>Anthocyanidins are a subclass of flavonoids. The most commonly consumed anthocyanidins are cyanidin, delphinidin, malvidin, pelargonidin, peonidin, and petunidin, found in foods such as berries and red fruits (<a href="#ref15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref15">Miguel, 2011</a>). Compared with other flavonoids, cyanidin is an effective anti-α-glucosidase agent (<a href="#ref31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref31">Tadera et al., 2006</a>). Glycosylation of anthocyanidins forms anthocyanins. Anthocyanins with 3-glucosides, such as cyanidin-3-glucosides, are the main anthocyanins found in berries (<a href="#ref2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref2">Chaves et al., 2018</a>). However, the ability of anthocyanins to inhibit α-glucosidase is decreased by glycosylation, and by substitution of different types of sugar (<a href="#ref35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref35">Wang et al., 2010</a>; <a href="#ref40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref40">Xu et al., 2018</a>). Previous studies indicated that a high intake of anthocyanidins and anthocyanin-rich fruits (e.g. berries, grapes, blackcurrants, apples, and cowpeas) was associated with lower risk of type II DM (<a href="#ref11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref11">Jenkins et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#ref36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref36">Wedick et al., 2012</a>). Results from animal models also suggested that plant extracts rich in anthocyanidins reduce high blood glucose levels and decrease hemoglobin A1c by enhancing insulin secretion and improving insulin resistance (<a href="#ref42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref42">Zang et al., 2016</a>). Specifically, cyanidin-3-glucoside was reported to reduce diabetes-associated hyperlipidemia in mouse fibroblast 3T3-L1 cells by suppressing hexosamine biosynthesis, FoxO1 and adipose triglyceride pathways (<a href="#ref7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref7">Guo et al., 2012</a>). In addition, delphinidin was shown to decrease glucose absorption <em>via</em> the free fatty acid receptor-1 dependent pathway in human intestinal cell lines (<a href="#ref9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref9">Hidalgo et al., 2017</a>). Thus, anthocyanins and anthocyanidins have potential to be used as nutritional bioactive compounds with therapeutic antidiabetic properties.</p>
<p>Limited information is available on the inhibitory activity of anthocyanidins on α-glucosidase, and on the inhibitory modes of anthocyanidins and their glycoside forms (anthocyanins). Thus, we investigated the inhibitory effects of anthocyanidins and their glycosides on α-glucosidase, including IC<sub>50</sub>, inhibitory constant (<em>K</em><sub>i</sub>), mode of inhibition, and structural interactions (molecular docking). Since high anthocyanidin contents are present in berries, strawberries, cherries, grapes, raspberries, red wine, tea, and fruit peel with dark pigments, the results of this research will be useful for development of functional foods with high anthocyanidin contents. Moreover, through using enzyme-inhibitor structural analysis, we described the molecular interactions between anthocyanidins as the templates and α-glucosidase as the key enzyme in type II DM prevention and treatment.</p></section><section id="sec2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2>
<section id="sec3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Chemicals and reagents</h3>
<p>The enzyme [<em>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</em> (<em>S. cerevisiae</em>) α-glucosidase (type 1, ≥10 unit/mg)] and substrate [<em>p</em>-nitrophenyla-D-glucopyranoside (<em>p</em>NPG)] were sourced from Sigma-Aldrich Co. (St. Louis, MO, USA). Standards, including cyanidin chloride (≥95%), malvidin chloride (≥ 95%), pelargonidin chloride (≥98%), peonidin chloride (≥95%), delphinidin chloride (≥95%), and petunidin chloride (≥98%), were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Co.. Delphin chloride (≥97%), delphinidin-3-galactoside chloride (≥95%), and delphinidin-3-glucoside chloride (≥95%) were purchased from Extrasynthese (Genay, Lyon, France).</p></section><section id="sec4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Enzyme assays for α-glucosidase inhibitory activity</h3>
<p>Inhibition of α-glucosidase was assessed in assays containing 5 μL of anthocyanins/anthocyanidins (various concentrations), 10 μL of <em>S. cerevisiae</em> α-glucosidase (0.01 U/mL), 25 μL of <em>p</em>NPG (1.25 mM) in 160 μL of 50 mM KPB (pH 6). The reaction was monitored at a wavelength of 405 nm at 37°C for 30 min using a Synergy<sup>TM</sup> HT 96-well UV-visible microplate reader with a Gen5 data analysis software program (BioTek Instruments, Inc., Winooski, VT, USA). Result was calculated as a percentage of inhibitory activity using the following equation:</p>
<table class="disp-formula p" id="M1"><tr><td class="formula"><math id="M4" display="block"><mrow><mrow><mi>Inhibition</mi><mo></mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mo>%</mo><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mfrac><mrow><msub><mrow><mi>V</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>oB</mi></mrow></msub><mo>-</mo><msub><mrow><mi>V</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>ob</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><mrow><msub><mrow><mi>V</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>oA</mi></mrow></msub><mo>-</mo><msub><mrow><mi>V</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>oa</mi></mrow></msub></mrow></mfrac><mo>×</mo><mn>100</mn></mrow></mrow></math></td></tr></table>
<p>where <em>V</em><sub>oA</sub> is the initial velocity of the control reaction with enzyme (control), <em>V</em><sub>oa</sub> is the initial velocity of the control reaction without enzyme (control blank), <em>V</em><sub>oB</sub> is the initial velocity of the enzyme reaction with sample, and <em>V</em><sub>ob</sub> is the initial velocity of the reaction with sample but without enzyme (sample blank).</p>
<p>The efficiency of anthocyanins and anthocyanidins for inhibiting α-glucosidase was determined by the IC<sub>50</sub>, analyzed using a dose-response plot of anthocyanins/anthocyanidins versus percentage of inhibition using a Graph-Pad Prism (version 5.00) for Windows (GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA, USA, <a href="https://www.graphpad.com" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">www.graphpad.com</a>).</p></section><section id="sec5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Enzyme kinetics of α-glucosidase inhibitory activity</h3>
<p>The <em>K</em><sub>i</sub> and mode of inhibition for inhibition of <em>S. cerevisiae</em> α-glucosidase (0.01 U/mL) by anthocyanins/anthocyanidins were determined using with 10 different concentrations of <em>p</em>NPG (0.1∼1.2 mM) in 50 mM KPB (pH 6). Assays were performed using the 96-well microplate reader as mentioned previously. Investigation of the kinetic behaviours of anthocyanins/anthocyanidins on α-glucosidase was performed using the Michaelis-Menten equation with a least squares fit parameter using the GraphPad Prism (version 5.00) for Windows (GraphPad Software). The Michaelis-Menten equation presents a relationship between substrate concentrations and reaction velocity and is expressed as:</p>
<table class="disp-formula p" id="disp-formula2"><tr><td class="formula"><math id="M2" display="block"><mrow><mrow><mi>V</mi><mo>=</mo><mfrac><mrow><msub><mrow><mi>V</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>max</mi></mrow></msub><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mi>S</mi><mo stretchy="false">]</mo></mrow><mrow><msub><mrow><mi>K</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>m</mi></mrow></msub><mo>+</mo><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mi>S</mi><mo stretchy="false">]’</mo></mrow></mfrac></mrow></mrow></math></td></tr></table>
<p>where <em>V</em> is the initial velocity of the reaction, <em>V</em><sub>max</sub> is the maximum velocity of reaction rate obtained with excess substrate and saturated enzyme, <em>K</em><sub>m</sub> is the Michaelis-Menten constant indicating a binding affinity of enzyme and substrate at 50% of the reaction rate, and <em>S</em> is the concentration of substrate.</p>
<p>The Lineweaver-Burk plot was generated using Graph-Pad Prism (version 5.00) for Windows by plotting the inverse of substrate concentration and reaction velocity to determine <em>V</em><sub>max</sub> and <em>K</em><sub>m</sub>. The intercept on the y-axis represents 1/<em>V</em><sub>max</sub>, the intercept on the X-axis indicates −1/<em>K</em><sub>m</sub>, and the slope is <em>K</em><sub>m</sub>/<em>V</em><sub>max</sub>. The Lineweaver-Burk plot is expressed as:</p>
<table class="disp-formula p" id="M3"><tr><td class="formula"><math id="M5" display="block"><mrow><mrow><mfrac><mrow><mn>1</mn></mrow><mrow><mi>V</mi></mrow></mfrac><mo>=</mo><mfrac><mrow><mn>1</mn></mrow><mrow><msub><mrow><mi>V</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>max</mi></mrow></msub></mrow></mfrac><mo>+</mo><mfrac><mrow><msub><mrow><mi>K</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>m</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><mrow><msub><mrow><mi>V</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>max</mi></mrow></msub></mrow></mfrac><mo>×</mo><mfrac><mrow><mn>1</mn></mrow><mrow><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mi>S</mi><mo stretchy="false">]</mo></mrow></mfrac></mrow></mrow></math></td></tr></table>
<p>Conversely, a Lineweaver-Burk double reciprocal plot was transformed from the Michaelis-Menten equation to determine the mode of inhibition. <em>K</em><sub>i</sub> was determined by the slope of the Lineweaver-Burk double reciprocal plot and the concentrations of anthocyanins and anthocyanidins.</p></section><section id="sec6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Molecular docking</h3>
<p>The X-ray crystallographic structure of α-glucosidase was retrieved from the Protein Data Bank (<a href="http://www.rcsb.org/pdb" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://www.rcsb.org/pdb</a>). Several human α-glucosidases, including the intestinal N-terminal maltase glucoamylase (PDB ID: 2QMJ), the C-terminal maltase glucoamylase (PDB ID: 3TOP), and the N-terminal sucrose isomaltase (PDB ID: 3LPP), have been submitted to and are available on the protein database. These proteins have been used for predicting binding interactions using molecular docking analysis in previous studies (<a href="#ref27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref27">Shang et al., 2013</a>; <a href="#ref22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref22">Priscilla et al., 2014</a>; <a href="#ref5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref5">Flores-Bocanegra et al., 2015</a>; <a href="#ref43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref43">Zhang et al., 2016</a>). The results showed that the N-terminal maltase gluco-amylase shared high structural similarity with the C-terminal maltase glucoamylase and the N-terminal sucrose isomaltase. Interestingly, the N-terminal maltase gluco-amylase was the most selective molecular model for studying α-glucosidase inhibition (<a href="#ref19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref19">Naumov, 2007</a>; <a href="#ref24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref24">Quezada-Calvillo et al., 2007</a>). Thus, the human intestinal N-terminal maltase glucoamylase was chosen as the template in this study.</p>
<p>All water molecules and miscellaneous molecules were removed from the receptor model using the Discovery Studio Client (version 2.5) program from Dassault Systèmes BIOVIA (San Diego, CA, USA) (<a href="#ref16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref16">Mikulic-Petkovsek et al., 2017</a>) to avoid an unclear binding pocket and distorted structural model. Hydrogen atoms and Gasteiger-Huckel charges were then added to the model to generate a stabilized chemical structure. The 3D structures of all flavonoids were downloaded from PubChem (<a href="https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov</a>) and computed using Sybyl Molecular Modeling Package (Tripos, Inc., St. Louis, MO, USA) (<a href="#ref34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref34">Vujović et al., 2017</a>). Binding energy was calculated using AutoDockTools (version 4.2, AutoDock, La Jolla, CA, USA) from The Scripps Research Institute (TSRI) (La Jolla, CA, USA) (<a href="#ref18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref18">Morris et al., 2009</a>) to predict the interaction between the α-glucosidase model and anthocyanins/anthocyanidins. The grid box size was 84× 83×81 Å in the x, y, and z dimensions, respectively, with a spacing of 0.375 Å. During this procedure, the genetic algorithm (GA) was set at 30 runs, and the Lamarckian GA was chosen for docking calculations. Analyses were performed using the data collection tool Cygwin Terminal (version 2.8.0) from Cygwin<sup>TM</sup> API library (<a href="https://cygwin.com" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://cygwin.com</a>) (<a href="#ref25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref25">Racine, 2000</a>). Text on molecular docking was edited using a text editor program, EditPlus (version 4.3, ES-Computing, Jinju, Korea, <a href="https://www.editplus.com" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://www.editplus.com</a>). Structural molecular analyses and molecular images were visualized using the UCSF Chimera package (version 1.11.2) from the Resource for Biocomputing, Visualization and Informatics, University of California, San Francisco (supported by NIH P41 RR-01081) (<a href="#ref21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref21">Pettersen et al., 2004</a>), and the PyMOL Molecular Graphics System (version 1.8) from Schrödinger, LLC. (New York, NY, USA) (<a href="https://www.pymol.org" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://www.pymol.org</a>) (<a href="#ref26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref26">Salam et al., 2014</a>). The optimal docked model was selected based on the lowest binding free energy being the most favorable binding on α-glucosidase.</p></section><section id="sec7"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Statistical analysis</h3>
<p>All experiments were carried out in triplicate with data expressed as mean±standard deviation (SD). All statistical analyses were evaluated using SPSS version 19 from IBM Corp. (New York, NY, USA). Unpaired sample <em>t</em>-test was performed to determine the significant differences between IC<sub>50</sub> values of anthocyanins/anthocyanidins and that of acarbose. Significant differences was defined at <em>P</em> &lt;0.05.</p></section></section><section id="sec8"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">RESULTS AND DISCUSSION</h2>
<section id="sec9"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Inhibition of α-glucosidase</h3>
<p>Anthocyanidins found in nature include cyanidin, delphinidin, malvidin, pelargonidin, peonidin, and petunidin (<a href="#ref15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref15">Miguel, 2011</a>). These anthocyanidins exhibited IC<sub>50</sub> values against α-glucosidase ranging from 4.11 to 54.69 μM, which are higher than the diabetic drug, acarbose (IC<sub>50</sub> of 0.53±0.06 μM) (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). The inhibitory actions of the anthocyanidins were in the following order of magnitude: delphinidin&gt; cyanidin&gt; petunidin&gt; peonidin&gt; pelargonidin&gt; malvidin. The chemical structure of delphinidin (three hydroxyl moieties on the B ring) caused delphinidin (IC<sub>50</sub> of 4.11±0.49 μM) to exhibit greater inhibitory activity than cyanidin and petunidin (IC<sub>50</sub> of 17.01±1.35 and 30.78±1.17 μM, respectively). Cyanidin and petunidin both have two hydroxyl moieties, which suggests that the presence of more hydroxyl moieties at C-3’, C-4’, and C-5’ of the B ring promotes inhibitory activity (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a> and <a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>). The hydroxyl moieties at C-3’ and C-4’ of the B ring of cyanidin exhibited higher inhibitory activity than hydroxyl moieties at the C-4’ and C-5’ of the B ring of petunidin (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a> and <a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>), suggesting a more significant role of hydroxyl moieties at C-3’ than at C-5’ of the B ring. The significance of hydroxyl moieties was also revealed in the chemical structures of peonidin and petunidin (IC<sub>50</sub> of 36.11±2.72 and 30.78±1.17 μM, respectively). Petunidin (hydroxyl moiety at C-5’ of the B ring) exhibited higher inhibitory activity than peonidin (no hydroxyl moiety at this position) (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a> and <a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>). Replacement of hydroxyl moieties with meth-oxyl groups decreased inhibitory activity, as observed in malvidin (IC<sub>50</sub> of 54.69±2.14 μM) (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). Malvidin is composed of two methoxyl moieties at C-3’ and C-5’ of the B ring, and exhibited lower inhibitory activity than petunidin and peonidin (methoxyl moiety at C-3’) (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a> and <a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>). Pelargonidin, which only has one hydroxyl moiety at C-4’ of the B ring, exhibited lower inhibitory activity than delphinidin, cyaniding, and petunidin, which all have two or three hydroxyl moieties in the B ring (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a> and <a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>). However, peonidin (methoxyl moiety at C-3’) exhibited higher inhibitory activity than pelargonidin (IC<sub>50</sub> of 50.35±4.00 μM) with hydrogen at C-3’. However, compared with malvidin (two methoxyl moieties at C-3’ and C-5’), pelargonidin exhibited greater inhibitory activity, suggesting that the presence of a methoxyl moiety at C-5’ decreased the inhibitory activity to a greater extent than when the methoxyl moiety is at the C-3’ position. The presence of methoxylation and hydroxylation also increased the inhibition of α-glucosidase. Previous studies have indicated that hydroxylation of the B ring of the flavonoid structure increases the inhibitory potential (IC<sub>50</sub>) on α-glucosidase (<a href="#ref13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref13">Kim et al., 2006</a>; <a href="#ref39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref39">Xiao and Högger, 2015</a>). Indeed, the presence of more hydroxyl groups enhanced inhibition of α-glucosidase (<a href="#ref6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref6">Gao and Kawabata, 2004</a>). However, the presence of methoxyl groups reduced inhibition of α-glucosidase (<a href="#ref38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref38">Xiao et al., 2013</a>).</p>

<figcaption><p>Chemical structures of anthocyanins and anthocyanidins used in this experiment.</p></figcaption></figure><p>Since delphinidin exhibited the highest inhibitory activity with the lowest IC<sub>50</sub> value, we further investigated its glycosides. Results suggested that all the glycosides (delphinidin-3-glucoside, delphinidin-3-galactoside, and delphin) were poorer inhibitors of α-glucosidase than delphinidin (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). Substitution of sugar moieties (glucose and galactose) at C3 on the C ring of delphinidin weakened the inhibitory effect on α-glucosidase. This was indicated by increased IC<sub>50</sub> values (delphinidin-3-glucoside, 364.32±15.19 μM; delphinidin-3-galactoside, 368.87 ±13.81 μM). Furthermore, delphinidin-3,5-diglucoside (delphin) was the weakest inhibitor of α-glucosidase since no inhibitory action was observed (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). These results imply that replacement of the hydroxyl group at C3 of the C ring by either glucose or galactose moieties lowered its potential for α-glucosidase inhibition. Moreover, addition of glucose at C3 of the C ring and C5 of the A ring of the delphinidin skeleton drastically decreased the inhibition of α-glucosidase, suggesting that steric hindrance of bulky structures negatively effects binding interactions between delphinidin glycosides and α-glucosidase.</p>
<p>A previous study showed that glycosylation of the flavonoid skeleton decreased inhibition of α-glucosidase (<a href="#ref35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref35">Wang et al., 2010</a>). The inhibitory activity was decreased to a greater extent with a larger number of glycoside substitutions on flavonoids (i.e. polyglycoside vs. monoglycoside residues) (<a href="#ref14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref14">Kim et al., 2000</a>). The increase in bulkiness and polarity of the chemical structure causes a steric effect, which tends to possess a non-planar structure, thus reducing the favorable binding interactions between the flavonoids and α-glucosidase (<a href="#ref37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref37">Xiao et al., 2010</a>). Other studies have indicated that the inhibitory activity of flavonoid glycosylation on α-glucosidase depended on the type, position, molecular size, and polarity of the sugar moiety, which is consistent with the results of our present study on anthocyanins and anthocyanidins (<a href="#ref12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref12">Kawabata et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#ref28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref28">Shibano et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#ref4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref4">Fan et al., 2010</a>; <a href="#ref38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref38">Xiao et al., 2013</a>).</p></section><section id="sec10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Enzyme kinetics and mode of inhibition</h3>
<p>Enzyme kinetics (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>) revealed that acarbose and petunidin behaved as competitive inhibitors of α-glucosidase, suggesting that the inhibitors competed with the substrate for the active site of the enzyme. Therefore, the inhibitor blocked the substrate from reaching the active site, thereby interfering with enzymatic activity. This finding was consistent with a previous report that indicated that acarbose inhibits in a competitive manner (<a href="#ref8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref8">Hansawasdi and Kawabata, 2006</a>). However, peonidin inhibited α-glucosidase in a non-competitive manner (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>), indicating binding to either the free enzyme or enzyme-substrate complex. Interactions occurring outside of the active site alter the structure of the enzyme and prevent the substrate from entering the active site. The other anthocyanidins and anthocyanins (with the exception of delphin) inhibited α-glucosidase in mixed-type, close to non-competitive inhibitors manner, suggesting that these compounds bind the free enzymes and enzyme-substrate complexes (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). The type of inhibition could not be determined for delphin (two molecules of glucose attached to C3 of the C ring and C5 of the A ring) due to low inhibitory activity.</p>
<p>The determination of <em>K</em><sub>i</sub> for the inhibition of α-glucosidase showed that anthocyanidins and anthocyanins had <em>K</em><sub>i</sub> value of the following order of magnitude: delphinidin&lt; peonidin&lt; petunidin&lt; cyanidin&lt; pelargonidin&lt; malvidin&lt; delphinidin-3-glucoside&lt; delphinidin-3-galactoside (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). A smaller <em>K</em><sub>i</sub> implies stronger inhibition or a higher affinity for binding α-glucosidase (tight binding). The <em>K</em><sub>i</sub> value also indicates the effectiveness of ligand binding to the enzyme. Based on these data, delphinidin had the strongest binding to α-glucosidase, whereas delphinidin-3-galactoside had the poorest (<em>K</em><sub>i</sub> of 0.078 μM and 98.69 μM, respectively). This finding was in good agreement with the IC<sub>50</sub> values (delphinidin had the lowest IC<sub>50</sub>, whereas delphinidin-3-galactoside had the highest IC<sub>50</sub>). Interestingly, delphinidin exhibited a similar <em>K</em><sub>i</sub> value as acarbose (<em>K</em><sub>i</sub>=0.069 μM), suggesting delphinidin is an effective anti-α-glucosidase agent.</p></section><section id="sec11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Molecular docking</h3>
<p>To understand the interactions between anthocyanidins, especially delphinidin, and α-glucosidase, a molecular docking technique was used to investigate docking of enzyme-anthocyanidin interactions, both on the centered macromolecule and the enzyme active site. We used the X-ray crystallographic structure of human intestinal N-terminal maltase glucoamylase as the macromolecule receptor to determine an optimally conformational position of anthocyanidins on α-glucosidase. Molecular docking was predicted by the scores of the lowest binding free energy to indicate potential interactions between anthocyanidins and α-glucosidase.</p>
<p>The docking results indicated that enzyme-anthocyanidins interactions throughout the macromolecule were not the same as interactions at the active site. A previous report suggested that docking analysis of the competitive inhibitor, acarbose, on the N-terminal subunit of maltase glucoamylase occurred between the catalytic triad (Asp327, Asp443, and Asp542) and active site residues (Thr205 and Arg526) (<a href="#ref30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref30">Sim et al., 2008</a>). Similar results were observed in our experiment (<a href="#T2" class="usa-link">Table 2</a>). However, the interaction between acarbose and the enzyme active site differed from that between acarbose and the entire enzyme, during which acarbose preferred forming hydrogen bonds with Gln272, Gln275, Asp649, His657, Glu658, Asp697, and Arg730 of the enzyme. Considering the chemical structure of acarbose on human α-glucosidase, the interaction mostly occurred through the hydroxyl moieties of acarbose (<a href="#ref38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref38">Xiao et al., 2013</a>; <a href="#ref23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="ref23">Proença et al., 2017</a>). The mode of inhibition of acarbose, the competitive inhibitor, corresponded with the free binding energy. Indeed, the free binding energy of docking to the whole enzyme (−0.91 kcal/mol) was higher than that of docking into the enzyme active site (−2.98 kcal/mol) (<a href="#T2" class="usa-link">Table 2</a>). Therefore, acarbose likely accessed the catalytic pocket of α-glucosidase and formed interactions with the active-site residues rather than interacting away from the active site.</p>

<div class="tw-foot p"><div class="fn" id="t2fn1"><p>Estimated free binding energy (EFBE) and interactions with enzyme ligands are presented for interactions centered on whole macromolecules and the enzyme active sites.</p></div></div></section><p>Structural prediction of molecular docking in the centered macromolecule of the enzyme to create a flexible binding mode indicated free binding energy scores ranging from −9.00 kcal/mol to −6.02 kcal/mol (<a href="#T2" class="usa-link">Table 2</a>). Molecular docking analysis showed that delphinidin exhibited the lowest estimated free binding energy (−9.00 kcal/mol), suggesting that this anthocyanidin has potential to form strong interactions with α-glucosidase. The results from our molecular docking analysis, especially the estimated free binding energy, correspond with results from enzyme kinetics, suggesting delphinidin with the lowest <em>K</em><sub>i</sub> and IC<sub>50</sub> values is a strong α-glucosidase inhibitor. Our structural predictions indicated that delphinidin forms six hydrogen bonds with enzyme residues located outside of the active site (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>). Indeed, the hydrogen atom on the hydroxyl moiety at C-3’ of the B ring interacts with oxygen atoms of the backbone carboxyl moiety of Phe535, while the oxygen atom at the C-4’ hydroxyl moiety directly interacts with hydrogen atoms on the backbone amine moiety of Ile523. In addition, the hydrogen atom of the C-5’ hydroxyl moiety of the B ring interacts with oxygen atoms of the backbone carboxyl moieties of Arg520 and Ser521, while oxygen atoms on the hydroxyl moieties at C-5 and C-7 of the A ring interact with hydrogen atoms of the backbone amine moieties of Asp777 and Thr778, respectively.</p>

Again, delphinidin had the lowest estimated free binding energy, and was predicted to form hydrogen bonds with enzyme residues inside the active site <em>via</em> Asp443, Arg526, Asp542, Gln603, and Tyr605 (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>). The hydrogen atom of the C-3 hydroxyl moiety of the C ring interacts with the oxygen atom on the γ-acidic side chain of Asp542. Moreover, the hydrogen atom of the C-5 hydroxyl moiety of the A ring interacts with the γ-acidic side chain of Asp443, whereas its oxygen atom interacts with the side chain η<sup>1</sup>-amine of Arg526.</p>

Potential interactions with enzyme residues are shown with dashed lines.</p></figcaption></figure><p>Through determining the free binding energy of delphinidin docking into the centered macromolecule and the catalytic pocket of the enzyme, we showed that delphinidin preferred to bind at a site far away from the active site of α-glucosidase (−9.00 kcal/mol) rather than within the active site (−8.38 kcal/mol). Interestingly, the free binding energy was in agreement with our kinetic results that indicated delphinidin is a mixed-type, close to non-competitive α-glucosidase inhibitor. Based on these results, we concluded that delphinidin prefers to interact with either the free enzyme or the enzyme-substrate complex, thus interfering with the enzyme-substrate intermediate. In addition, molecular docking analysis confirmed that delphinidin acts as a mixed-type inhibition as it interacts with enzyme residues in the region of the enzyme located far away from the active sites. The structure-activity relationship suggests that 3’, 4’, and 5’ trihydroxyl moieties on the B ring play important roles in the inhibitory activity of delphinidin as these trihydroxyl moieties form hydrogen bonds with α-glucosidase. Moreover, the presence of the hydroxyl group may be essential to maintain interactions between delphinidin and α-glucosidase, which retard the entrance of substrates into the enzyme active site.</p>
<p>These data suggest that delphinidin is a potent α-glucosidase mixed-type, close to non-competitive inhibitor. Therefore, delphinidin shows potential as an effective anti-α-glucosidase agent and future diabetic drug. However, its bioaccessibility, toxicity and ability to inhibit α-glucosidase activity <em>in vitro</em> and <em>in vivo</em> are currently unknown.</p></section></section><section id="ack1" class="ack"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">ACKNOWLEDGEMENTS</h2>
