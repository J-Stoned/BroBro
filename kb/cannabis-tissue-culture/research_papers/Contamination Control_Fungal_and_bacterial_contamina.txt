<p>Auxin modulates sucrose transport to repress petal abscission in rose, and this process is regulated by a <em>Rh</em>ARF7-<em>Rh</em>SUC2 module in the abscission zone.</p></section><section class="abstract" id="abstract2"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>Developmental transitions in plants require adequate carbon resources, and organ abscission often occurs due to competition for carbohydrates/assimilates. Physiological studies have indicated that organ abscission may be activated by Suc deprivation; however, an underlying regulatory mechanism that links Suc transport to organ shedding has yet to be identified. Here, we report that transport of Suc and the phytohormone auxin to petals through the phloem of the abscission zone (AZ) decreases during petal abscission in rose (<em>Rosa hybrida</em>), and that auxin regulates Suc transport into the petals. Expression of the Suc transporter Rh<em>SUC2</em> decreased in the AZ during rose petal abscission. Similarly, silencing of Rh<em>SUC2</em> reduced the Suc content in the petals and promotes petal abscission. We established that the auxin signaling protein <em>Rh</em>ARF7 binds to the promoter of Rh<em>SUC2</em>, and that silencing of Rh<em>ARF7</em> reduces petal Suc contents and promotes petal abscission. Overexpression of Rh<em>SUC2</em> in the petal AZ restored accelerated petal abscission caused by Rh<em>ARF7</em> silencing. Moreover, treatment of rose petals with auxin and Suc delayed ethylene-induced abscission, whereas silencing of Rh<em>ARF7</em> and Rh<em>SUC2</em> accelerated ethylene-induced petal abscission. Our results demonstrate that auxin modulates Suc transport during petal abscission, and that this process is regulated by a <em>Rh</em>ARF7-<em>Rh</em>SUC2 module in the AZ.</p></section><section id="s1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">INTRODUCTION</h2>
<p>Abscission, a common process in plants, involves the detachment of organs from the main body and is triggered by developmental and environmental cues (<a href="#bib5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib5">Bleecker and Patterson, 1997</a>; <a href="#bib54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib54">Roberts et al., 2002</a>; <a href="#bib27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib27">Lewis et al., 2006</a>; <a href="#bib57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib57">Sawicki et al., 2015</a>; <a href="#bib65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib65">Tucker and Kim, 2015</a>). Organ abscission often occurs due to lack of nutrients and competition for carbohydrates (<a href="#bib66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib66">van Doorn, 2002</a>). Carbohydrate transport is thus thought to play a critical role in regulating abscission.</p>
<figure class="fig xbox font-sm" id="fx1"><p class="img-box line-height-none margin-x-neg-2 tablet:margin-x-0 text-center"><img class="graphic" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/4022/7610287/d4a9abb0bad9/TPC_201900695R2_fx1.jpg" loading="lazy" height="2190" width="3500" alt="graphic file with name TPC_201900695R2_fx1.jpg"></p>
<div class="p text-right font-secondary"><a href="figure/fx1/" class="usa-link" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Open in a new tab</a></div></figure><p>Suc can serve as the principal long-distance transport form of carbohydrates and energy (<a href="#bib53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib53">Riesmeier et al., 1994</a>). Suc distribution between source and sink organ depends on the sink strength, which in turn is determined by competition for nutrition between different organs (<a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Marcelis et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#bib57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib57">Sawicki et al., 2015</a>; <a href="#bib73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib73">Yu et al., 2015</a>). Plants sense Suc distribution, and the associated signaling pathways regulate development. As an example in Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>), Suc accelerates the transition from the juvenile to the adult stage by reducing levels of the micro-RNA miR156 (<a href="#bib72" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib72">Yu et al., 2013</a>). In pea (<em>Pisum sativum</em>), Suc is considered as the initial regulator of apical dominance (<a href="#bib35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib35">Mason et al., 2014</a>), and in rose (<em>Rosa hybrida</em>), Suc mediates the light-mediated control of bud burst (<a href="#bib18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib18">Henry et al., 2011</a>). A major decline in Suc levels in abscising organs has been observed in several plant species, including rose (<a href="#bib6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib6">Borochov et al., 1976</a>), pepper (<em>Capsicum annuum</em>; <a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Aloni et al., 1997</a>), <em>citrus</em> (<a href="#bib13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib13">Gómez-Cadenas et al., 2000</a>), and apple (<em>Malus domestica</em>; <a href="#bib76" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib76">Zhu et al., 2011</a>). However, the significance of a reduced Suc supply and the mechanism by which it is regulated have not been characterized.</p>
<p>Suc translocation is mainly mediated by two Suc transporter families: Suc Carrier or Suc Transporter (SUC/SUT), and Sugar Will Eventually be Exported Transporter (SWEET). SUC proteins function as the principal mediators of long-distance Suc transport, while SWEET proteins mainly play roles in Suc loading and unloading (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Chen et al., 2012</a>; <a href="#bib11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib11">Eom et al., 2015</a>). SUC proteins have 12 transmembrane domains (<a href="#bib25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib25">Lalonde et al., 2004</a>) and are classified into 3 types, based on their structure and function (<a href="#bib52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib52">Reinders et al., 2012</a>; <a href="#bib50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib50">Peng et al., 2014</a>). Dicot-specific Type I and monocot-specific Type IIB members are associated with Suc loading and transport (<a href="#bib56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib56">Sauer, 2007</a>), whereas Type III proteins localize to the tonoplast and plasma membrane and are thought to facilitate Suc release (<a href="#bib49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib49">Payyavula et al., 2011</a>). In Arabidopsis, the Type I member SUC2 localizes to the phloem and plays a key role in Suc loading and long-distance transport. In agreement, mutation of Arabidopsis <em>SUC2</em> results in an impaired nutrition phenotype, as evidenced by stunted growth and the accumulation of carbohydrates in source organs (<a href="#bib15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib15">Gottwald et al., 2000</a>). However, to date SUC proteins have not been shown to be involved in organ abscission.</p>
<p>The abscission process is tightly regulated by endogenous phytohormones, with auxin acting as a major inhibitor of abscission and ethylene as an accelerator (<a href="#bib24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib24">La Rue, 1936</a>; <a href="#bib54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib54">Roberts et al., 2002</a>; <a href="#bib32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib32">Ma et al., 2015</a>). A continuous polar auxin flow passing through the abscission zone (AZ) inhibits abscission, and auxin depletion in the AZ results in abscission initiation as a consequence of enhancing the sensitivity of the AZ to ethylene (<a href="#bib24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib24">La Rue, 1936</a>; <a href="#bib54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib54">Roberts et al., 2002</a>; <a href="#bib32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib32">Ma et al., 2015</a>). In addition, ethylene sensitivity can be significantly reduced by auxin during organ ripening and detachment (<a href="#bib58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib58">Sexton and Roberts, 1982</a>; <a href="#bib45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib45">Olsson and Butenko, 2018</a>; <a href="#bib60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib60">Shin et al., 2019</a>). The regulatory genes involved in the auxin and ethylene signal transduction pathways have been shown to be involved in the regulation of abscission, and include <em>AUXIN RESPONSE FACTOR</em> (<em>ARF</em>)<em>1</em>, <em>ARF2</em>, <em>ARF7</em>, and <em>ARF19</em> (<a href="#bib10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib10">Ellis et al., 2005</a>; <a href="#bib31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib31">Lombardi et al., 2015</a>), and the ethylene signaling gene <em>ETHYLENE RESPONSE 1</em> (<em>ETR1</em>) and <em>ETHYLENE INSENSITIVE 2</em> (<a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Patterson and Bleecker, 2004</a>). However, many aspects of the interaction between auxin and ethylene in organ abscission are not well understood.</p>
<p>Here, we investigate the regulatory mechanism of Suc transport during rose petal abscission. We show that auxin regulates Suc transport during petal abscission, and that a decrease in Suc transport enhances ethylene sensitivity of the petal AZ. The molecular mechanism regulating this process is described.</p></section><section id="s2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">RESULTS</h2>
<section id="s3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Auxin Regulates Suc Transport during Petal Abscission</h3>
<p>To investigate the roles of Suc and auxin in rose petal abscission, we chose an abscission-prone rose cultivar (<em>Rosa hybrida</em> cv Golden Shower) and divided the process of flower opening and abscission into 6 stages (<a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Gao et al., 2016</a>; <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 1</a>). As determined by scanning electron microscopy, petal AZ cells appeared to be more densely packed than adjacent cells from stage 1 to stage 3, with looser packing from stage 4 to stage 5 and clear intercellular separation at stage 6 (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 1</a>). Accordingly, we selected stage 3 and stage 5 as marker stages before and after the initiation of abscission, respectively.</p>
<p>To gain a better understanding of Suc dynamics during petal abscission, we used the fluorescent coumarin glucoside esculin, which is recognized by Suc transporters (<a href="#bib7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib7">Chandran et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#bib14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib14">Gora et al., 2012</a>; <a href="#bib21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib21">Knox and Oparka, 2018</a>), to simulate the transport of Suc through the petal AZ. Images of longitudinal sections showed that the strength of the esculin signal in the area from the AZ to the petal was lower at stage 5 compared to stage 3 (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figure 1A</a>). Transverse sections of petal AZs also revealed a much weaker esculin signal in the phloem of the AZ at stage 5 relative to stage 3 (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figure 1A</a>). To investigate auxin distribution during petal abscission, we performed immunolocalization studies of longitudinal sections with an anti-indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) monoclonal antibody, and observed a lower immunofluorescence signal in the area from the AZ to petal at stage 5 when compared to stage 3 (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figure 1B</a>). Liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS)/MS analysis of endogenous Suc and auxin contents in the AZ confirmed that their abundance significantly decreased in the AZ at stage 5 compared with stage 3 (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figures 1C and 1D</a>).</p>

Following Suc treatment, the time from fully opened flowers (stage 5) to complete petal abscission was 7.6 ± 0.5 d, and 5.0 ± 0.7 d for the mock control (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figures 1E and 1F</a>). To test whether this repressive effect of Suc was dependent on Suc hydrolysis or osmosis, we treated flowers with Glc or Fru as metabolic controls, and with mannitol as an osmotic control sugar (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figures 2A and 2B</a>). Suc application caused the most substantial delay in petal abscission relative to the other treatments, although Glc also caused a delay due to an increased energy supply (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figures 2A and 2B</a>). Treatment with the synthetic auxin naphthalene acetic acid (NAA) at 10 μM or 100 μM caused petals to wilt, but they barely abscised by the end of the experiment (15 d; <a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figures 1E and 1F</a>; <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 3</a>). By contrast, flowers treated with the auxin transport inhibitor naphthalene acetic acid (NPA) showed accelerated petal abscission, with only 3.0 ± 0.7 d from fully opened flowers to complete petal abscission (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figures 1E and 1F</a>). Notably, following a combined treatment with Suc and NPA, the time from fully opened flowers to abscission of all petals was 5.0 ± 0.7 d, which was similar to the mock treatment (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figures 1E and 1F</a>). These results therefore strongly suggest that Suc and auxin act in the same pathway in petal abscission. We also tested petal abscission in response to the auxin signaling inhibitor auxinole, which binds the auxin receptors TRANSPORT INHIBITOR RESPONSE1/AUXIN-SIGNALING F-BOX to inhibit auxin-responsive gene expression (<a href="#bib16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib16">Hayashi et al., 2012</a>). The flowers treated with auxinole exhibited accelerated petal abscission (average time to abscission 4.6 ± 0.5 d), whereas a combined treatment with Suc and auxinole restored the accelerated petal abscission by auxinole alone (average time to abscission 7.2 ± 1.3 d; <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 3</a>).</p>
<p>To further investigate the interaction between auxin and Suc during petal abscission, we determined the effects of NAA treatment on the fluorescence signal resulting from staining with esculin. Images of longitudinal and transverse sections showed a much higher esculin signal after NAA treatment compared to the signal in stage 5 flowers without NAA treatment (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figure 1A</a>). In addition, the IAA signal was similar to the mock control after Suc treatment (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figure 1B</a>). These results suggest that auxin regulates Suc transport during petal abscission, but that Suc does not affect the distribution of auxin.</p></section><section id="s4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Repression of Suc Transport by Silencing of Rh<em>SUC2</em> Promotes Petal Abscission</h3>
<p>To begin to elucidate the molecular mechanism behind long-distance Suc transport in the petal AZ, we identified three members of the rose <em>SUC</em> gene family (Rh<em>SUC2</em>, Rh<em>SUC3</em>, and Rh<em>SUC4</em>), all of which have been previously reported to be expressed in rose flowers (<a href="#bib18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib18">Henry et al., 2011</a>). We examined the expression patterns of these <em>SUC</em> genes in the petal AZ during flower opening and abscission by real time quantitative RT-PCR (RT-qPCR). Rh<em>SUC2</em> expression peaked at stage 3, before significantly decreasing at stage 5 (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 4A</a>), whereas the expression of Rh<em>SUC3</em> and Rh<em>SUC4</em> remained constant during flower opening and abscission (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 4A</a>). An analysis of Rh<em>SUC2</em> expression in specific tissues further revealed a more than twofold decrease in expression in the petal AZ at stage 5 compared to stage 3, whereas the adjacent petal and receptacle tissues experienced no such change (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2A</a>). In addition, NAA treatment significantly induced the expression of Rh<em>SUC2</em> in the petal AZ (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2B</a>). We therefore selected Rh<em>SUC2</em> for further analysis of its potential function in petal abscission.</p>

<p>We then tested the consequences of Rh<em>SUC2</em> silencing using virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS). To this end, we inserted 534 bp of the Rh<em>SUC2</em> 3′ untranslated region (UTR) into the pTRV2 VIGS vector. The Rh<em>SUC2</em>-silencing construct reduced the expression of Rh<em>SUC2</em> in transformed petals compared to the tobacco rattle virus (TRV) empty vector control (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figure 3A</a>), whereas it did not alter the expression of the related genes Rh<em>SUC3</em> or Rh<em>SUC4</em> (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 4B</a>), confirming the specificity of the gene silencing. To further examine whether the decrease of Suc levels in the Rh<em>SUC2</em>-silenced petals was caused by suppression of Suc transport, we simulated the transport of Suc using esculin. We observed a dramatic decrease in esculin transport into the petals of the silenced plants compared to the TRV control (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figure 3E</a>). Collectively, these results indicated that Suc transport is associated with petal development and abscission.</p>

<em>Rh</em>ARF7 Modulates Suc Transport by Regulating Rh<em>SUC2</em> Expression</h3>
<p>To investigate the regulation of Rh<em>SUC2</em>, we analyzed 1773 bp of the Rh<em>SUC2</em> promoter region upstream from the transcription start site using plant <em>cis</em>-acting regulatory DNA elements (PLACE) database (<a href="https://www.dna.affrc.go.jp/PLACE/?action=newplace" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://www.dna.affrc.go.jp/PLACE/?action=newplace</a>). This analysis revealed a number of <em>cis</em>-elements, including three auxin responsive <em>cis</em>-elements, AuxRE (TGTCTC), which are typical binding sites for auxin response factor (ARF) proteins (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figure 5A</a>). We also analyzed the promoter regions of <em>SUC2</em> in other plant species, including Arabidopsis, strawberry (<em>Fragaria vesca</em>), and peach (<em>Prunus persica</em>), and found that they all contained AuxRE elements (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figure 5A</a>). We next identified <em>ARF</em> genes in the rose genome database (<a href="https://lipm-browsers.toulouse.inra.fr/pub/RchiOBHm-V2/" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://lipm-browsers.toulouse.inra.fr/pub/RchiOBHm-V2/</a>) with similarity to those reported as regulators of petal abscission in Arabidopsis (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 7A</a>; <a href="#bib10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib10">Ellis et al., 2005</a>). We cross-validated this gene list against their expression patterns in the rose petal AZ. Of the 7 candidate <em>ARFs</em>, the expression of Rh<em>ARF7</em> and Rh<em>ARF8</em> increased in the petal AZ by over twofold at stage 3 relative to stage 1 and decreased sharply at stage 5 and stage 6 (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figure 5B</a>), thereby following an expression pattern similar to that of Rh<em>SUC2</em> during petal abscission (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2A</a>). We further tested the expression of all Rh<em>ARF</em> members in response to Suc treatment, and did not find any with greater than twofold change in expression (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figure 5C</a>), indicating that Suc does not substantially alter their expression.</p>

LC-MS/MS analysis confirmed the lower Suc content of Rh<em>ARF7-</em>silenced petals relative to the TRV control (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figure 3D</a>).</p></section><section id="s6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Rh<em>SUC2</em> Expression Rescues Accelerated Petal Abscission Caused by Rh<em>ARF7</em> Silencing</h3>
<p>To further dissect the genetic interaction between Rh<em>SUC2</em> and Rh<em>ARF7</em>, we silenced both Rh<em>SUC2</em> and Rh<em>ARF7</em> (TRV-Rh<em>SUC2</em>+Rh<em>ARF7</em>) in rose plants using VIGS, and separately transiently overexpressed Rh<em>SUC2</em> in the petal AZ of the Rh<em>ARF7</em>-silenced background (TRV-Rh<em>ARF7</em>+Rh<em>SUC2</em>OX). <p>In TRV-Rh<em>ARF7</em>+Rh<em>SUC2</em>OX plants, the expression of Rh<em>SUC2</em> increased 3.2 times over that seen in Rh<em>ARF7</em>-silenced plants (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figure 3A</a>). This time to complete petal shedding was not significantly different from that of the TRV control (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figures 3B and 3C</a>).</p></section><section id="s7"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Silencing of Rh<em>SUC2</em> and Rh<em>ARF7</em> Increases in Petal Sensitivity to Ethylene</h3>
<p>Ethylene is the main accelerator of petal abscission. To examine the influence of Suc and auxin on ethylene-induced rose petal abscission, we therefore treated flowers with Suc and auxin in an air-tight chamber containing 10 ppm ethylene. This treatment delayed ethylene-induced petal abscission compared to the mock control, and a combination of Suc and auxin treatments had a greater retardation effect compared to either treatment alone (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6A</a>; <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Video</a>). When we tested the effects of Rh<em>SUC2</em> and Rh<em>ARF7</em> silencing on ethylene-induced petal abscission, we observed that the time of petal abscission following ethylene treatment in Rh<em>SUC2</em>- and Rh<em>ARF7</em>-silenced plants was 10.3 ± 2.1 h and 7.6 ± 1.0 h, respectively, compared with 21.5 ± 2.5 h for the TRV control plants (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6B</a>). These results indicate that Suc and auxin repress ethylene sensitivity during petal abscission.</p>

<p>The shedding of superfluous organs is necessary for normal plant development and survival (<a href="#bib47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib47">Patterson, 2001</a>), and the abscission process often occurs due to deprivation of, or competition for, carbohydrates/assimilates (<a href="#bib1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib1">Addicott and Lynch, 1955</a>). Here, we report the role and regulatory mechanism of Suc transport during petal abscission in rose flowers.</p>
<p>The exogenous application of Suc is well-known to suppress organ abscission or senescence in plants. In <em>Dendrobium</em> and pepper, Suc feeding was reported to inhibit flower abscission (<a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Aloni et al., 1997</a>; <a href="#bib46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib46">Pattaravayo et al., 2013</a>), and in <em>citrus</em>, branch girdling (that is, the removal of the bark and phloem) can lead to an increase in sugar content and a reduction in fruit abscission rates (<a href="#bib20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib20">Iglesias et al., 2006</a>), whereas defoliation reduces carbohydrate levels and increases fruit abscission (<a href="#bib36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib36">Mehouachi et al., 1995</a>). In this study, we determined the dynamics of Suc transport during petal abscission in vivo, and showed that Suc import into petals was impaired during petal abscission (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figures 1A and 1C</a>). Sugar transport includes symplastic and apoplastic pathways, with Suc transporters mainly functioning in the apoplastic pathway. SUC proteins with high-affinity Suc activity serve in phloem loading, long-distance transport, and unloading (<a href="#bib56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib56">Sauer, 2007</a>; <a href="#bib23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib23">Kühn and Grof, 2010</a>). The Arabidopsis SUC2 transporter belongs to the high Suc-affinity type and plays an essential role in Suc transport and distribution between source and sink tissues (<a href="#bib9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib9">Durand et al., 2018</a>). In the context of rose bud growth, Rh<em>SUC2</em>, Rh<em>SUC3</em>, and Rh<em>SUC4</em> have been reported to be expressed in buds. However, only Rh<em>SUC2</em> transcript levels displayed an upregulation in response to light in the buds of beheaded plants, hinting at the special role of Rh<em>SUC2</em> in light-induced bud break (<a href="#bib18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib18">Henry et al., 2011</a>). Here, we also observed the downregulation of Rh<em>SUC2</em> expression during petal abscission, whereas the expression of Rh<em>SUC3</em> and Rh<em>SUC4</em> did not change (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 4A</a>). Notably, Rh<em>SUC2</em> expression exhibited a downregulation in the AZ but not in the receptacle or petals (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2A</a>). Moreover, Rh<em>SUC2</em> silencing led to decreased Suc import to the petals and promoted petal abscission (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figure 3</a>). Taken together, these results demonstrate that <em>Rh</em>SUC2 plays a role in the regulation of petal abscission, in addition to bud outgrowth.</p>
<p>Auxin is a well-known inhibitor of abscission, and its depletion is a prerequisite for abscission (<a href="#bib31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib31">Lombardi et al., 2015</a>). In Arabidopsis, overexpression of an auxin synthesis gene in the AZ was reported to effectively reduce petal abscission (<a href="#bib4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib4">Basu et al., 2013</a>), and in tomato (<em>Solanum lycopersicum</em>), depletion of auxin by the removal of flowers resulted in promoting pedicel abscission (<a href="#bib37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib37">Meir et al., 2010</a>). In our study, auxin distribution decreased from the receptacle to the petal at stage 5 compared to stage 3 in rose (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figures 1B and 1D</a>), and auxin application inhibited petal abscission (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figures 1E and 1F</a>).</p>
<p>The influence of Suc on auxin metabolism and signaling has been described in previous studies. In Arabidopsis, Suc application led to elevated auxin levels and increased polar auxin transport in seedlings (<a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Lilley et al., 2012</a>). Suc has also been shown to promote auxin signaling to regulate the iron-deficiency response in Arabidopsis (<a href="#bib29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib29">Lin et al., 2016</a>). In maize (<em>Zea mays</em>), sugar levels regulate auxin biosynthesis in developing seeds (<a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">LeClere et al., 2010</a>), and in rose, Suc regulates bud outgrowth as an early modulator of relative phytohormone content during bud development (<a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Barbier et al., 2015</a>). In particular, the expression of genes encoding auxin biosynthesis and efflux carriers was induced during bud outgrowth (<a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Barbier et al., 2015</a>). However, we observed that Suc had no effect on the distribution of auxin during petal abscission (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figure 1B</a>), whereas auxin promoted Suc transport during abscission (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figure 1A</a>), indicating that auxin regulates Suc distribution during petal abscission. The expression of Rh<em>SUC2</em> was reported to be reduced in response to auxin in rose buds (<a href="#bib18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib18">Henry et al., 2011</a>), but we saw an upregulation in Rh<em>SUC2</em> expression by auxin in the petal AZ (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2B</a>). These differences may reflect distinct auxin-Suc regulatory relationships during bud outgrowth and petal abscission. Moreover, we observed that flower diameter and petal size of fully opened flowers were smaller in both Rh<em>SUC2</em>- and Rh<em>ARF7</em>-silenced plants compared to TRV control (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 5</a>). Previous studies suggested that defects in Suc transport or auxin signaling resulted in smaller size of flower. In Arabidopsis, the sizes of petals were significantly reduced in the <em>arf6 arf8</em> double mutant (<a href="#bib40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib40">Nagpal et al., 2005</a>). In cucumber (<em>Cucumis sativus</em>), silencing of the Suc transporter Cs<em>SUT1</em> resulted in smaller male flowers (<a href="#bib61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib61">Sun et al., 2019</a>).</p>
<p>It has been reported that ARF1, ARF2, ARF7, and ARF19 contribute to petal abscission in Arabidopsis (<a href="#bib10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib10">Ellis et al., 2005</a>; <a href="#bib44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib44">Okushima et al., 2005</a>), whereas we found that <em>Rh</em>ARF7 is involved in the regulation of petal abscission in rose (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figure 3</a>), and directly regulates the expression of Rh<em>SUC2</em> (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figures 5D to 5G</a>). Sequence analysis identified auxin responsive <em>cis</em>-elements (AuxRE) in the <em>SUC2</em> promoter that are conserved in the promoters of homologs from Arabidopsis, strawberry, and peach (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figure 5A</a>). This suggests that the regulation of Suc transport in the AZ by auxin may be a common phenomenon in plants.</p>
<p>The balance of auxin and ethylene constitutes a major organ abscission regulatory module in plants (<a href="#bib47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib47">Patterson, 2001</a>), with auxin inhibiting ethylene sensitivity (<a href="#bib63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib63">Taylor and Whitelaw, 2001</a>). However, the mechanistic details of this antagonistic mechanism remain unknown. Previous studies had demonstrated that sugar can influence ethylene metabolism and signaling. In carnation (<em>Dianthus caryophyllus</em>), Suc delayed the senescence of cut flowers by delaying ethylene biosynthesis and regulating ethylene sensitivity (<a href="#bib67" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib67">Verlinden and Garcia, 2004</a>; <a href="#bib19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib19">Hoeberichts et al., 2007</a>; <a href="#bib51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib51">Pun et al., 2016</a>). In Arabidopsis, Glc enhanced the degradation of the transcription factor ETHYLENE INSENSITIVE 3, a key regulator in ethylene signaling (<a href="#bib74" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib74">Zhou et al., 1998</a>; <a href="#bib59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib59">Shi et al., 2003</a>). Our results showed that Suc treatment can suppress ethylene-induced petal abscission in rose (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6A</a>; <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Video</a>), and that the strongest suppression of ethylene-induced petal abscission resulted from a cotreatment with auxin and Suc (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6A</a>; <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Video</a>). These results indicate that Suc acts as a mediator of the interaction between auxin and ethylene in organ abscission. Intriguingly, we observed that ethylene-induced abscission can be accelerated by silencing of Rh<em>SUC2</em> or Rh<em>ARF7</em> (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6B</a>). Our results suggest that auxin and Suc may have other functions in petal abscission, bypassing ethylene sensitivity during abscission.</p>
<p>Ethylene was reported to affect Suc distribution and accumulation as a feedback regulation. In carnation, ethylene promotes Suc mobilization from petals to other organs in the flower (<a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Nichols and Ho, 1975</a>), whereas in rice (<em>Oryza sativa</em>), ethylene inhibitors enhance Suc biosynthesis during grain filling (<a href="#bib41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib41">Naik and Mohapatra, 2000</a>). Moreover, ETHYLENE RESPONSIVE FACTOR 72 (ERF72) suppresses Suc biosynthesis in cassava (<em>Manihot esculenta</em> Crantz; <a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Liu et al., 2018</a>). Here, we showed that an ethylene treatment reduced Suc transport in the petal AZ (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6D</a>), but did not affect auxin distribution (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6E</a>), suggesting that ethylene reduces Suc transport as part of a feedback mechanism during petal abscission.</p>
<p>In conclusion, our results demonstrate that auxin induces Suc transport to repress petal abscission in the early stages of flower development. The reduction of auxin levels in the petal AZ leads to an attenuation of Suc transport and promotes the initiation of petal abscission. In this process, the <em>Rh</em>ARF7-<em>Rh</em>SUC2 module mediates the regulation by auxin of Suc transport (<a href="#fig7" class="usa-link">Figure 7</a>).</p>

<section id="s10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Plant Materials and Growth Conditions</h3>
<p>We used the abscission-prone rose (<em>Rosa hybrida</em> cv Golden Shower) as material for the experiments in this study, except for the VIGS assays. We harvested flowers at flower opening stage 2 from plants grown in a mixture of vermiculite and nutritive soil (1:1) in a greenhouse at the China Agricultural University (Beijing, China), and transported them to the laboratory within 1 h. We cut the stems again to a length of 16 cm under water and immediately placed them in distilled water. Vase solutions contained 200 mg L<sup>−1</sup> 8-hydroxyquinoline sulfate as anti-microbial agent.</p>
<p>Preliminary tests demonstrated that <em>R. hybrida</em> cv Samantha shows a high VIGS silencing efficiency and tissue-cultured <em>R. hybrida</em> cv Samantha plants can bloom in 40 d under our conditions. In addition, vase life experiments showed that <em>R. hybrida</em> cv Samantha and <em>R. hybrida</em> cv Golden Shower behaved similarly with respect to auxin and Suc treatments, such that petal abscission was inhibited by auxin, and delayed by Suc (<a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure 3</a>). We therefore used <em>Rosa hybrida</em> cv Samantha for VIGS assays. <em>R. hybrida</em> cv Samantha plants were propagated by tissue culture as previously described by <a href="#bib71" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib71">Wu et al. (2017)</a>. We used <em>Nicotiana benthamiana</em> plants as material for transient expression assays. <em>N. benthamiana</em> and rose plants were grown in a mixture of vermiculite and nutritive soil (1:1) at 23 ± 1°C, 40% to 60% relative humidity, and 100–120 μmol m<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> illumination with fluorescent lamps (SINOL, SN-T5, 16W) under a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod.</p>
<p>We conducted all flower longevity analyses at 23 ± 1°C, 60% to 80% relative humidity, 50–60 μmol m<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> illumination with fluorescent lamps under a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod. The phenotype of the flowers was photographed every day or monitored by time-lapse image capture.</p>
<p>The distal and proximal sides of the petal AZ are connected to the petal and receptacle, respectively. We collected petal AZ samples by excising both sides at the base of the petals (&lt;2 mm in length) and the area of the receptacles adjacent to the petals (&lt;2 mm in length; <a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Gao et al., 2016</a>).</p></section><section id="s11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Quantification of Endogenous Suc and Auxin Levels</h3>
<p>We measured Suc levels as previously described by <a href="#bib33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib33">Ma et al. (2017)</a>. Briefly, we ground freeze-dried petals or AZs using a mixer mill with zirconia beads for 1.5 min at 30 Hz. We then extracted 100 mg powder overnight at 4°C with 1 mL 70% (v/v) methanol. Following centrifugation at 10,000<em>g</em> for 10 min, we filtered the supernatants through a 0.22 μm membrane filter (SCAA-104, 0.22 μm pore size, ANPEL) for LC-MS/MS analysis.</p>
<p>For auxin extraction, we collected 120 mg fresh rose AZ tissue, followed by flash-freezing and grinding in liquid nitrogen, before extraction with 1.2 mL methanol/water (8/2, v/v) at 4°C overnight. We centrifuged the extract at 12,000<em>g</em> at 4°C for 15 min. We collected the supernatant and allowed it to evaporate to dryness under a nitrogen gas stream. We dissolved the final pellet in 100 μL methanol/water (3/7, v/v), centrifuged the solution at 12,000<em>g</em> at 4°C for 15 min, and collected the supernatant for LC-MS/MS analysis.</p>
<p>We performed LC-MS/MS using an LC-electrospray ionization (ESI)-MS/MS system (HPLC, Shim-pack UFLC SHIMADZU CBM30A system; MS, Applied Biosystems 6500 Triple Quadrupole). HPLC parameters were as follows: column, Waters ACQUITY UPLC HSS T3 C18 (1.8 µm, 2.1 mm*100 mm); solvent system, water (0.04% [v/v] acetic acid) and acetonitrile (0.04% [v/v] acetic acid); gradient program, 95:5 (v/v) at 0 min, 5:95 (v/v) at 11.0 min, 5:95 (v/v) at 12.0 min, 95:5 (v/v) at 12.1 min, 95:5 v/v at 15.0 min; flow rate, 0.4 mL min<sup>−1</sup> for Suc and 0.35 mL min<sup>−1</sup> for auxin; temperature, 40°C; injection volume: 2 μL for Suc and 5 μL for auxin. The effluent was alternatively connected to an ESI-triple quadrupole-linear ion trap-MS. The ESI source operation parameters were as follows: ion source, turbo spray; source temperature 500°C; ion spray voltage (IS) 5500 V; curtain gas (CUR) were set at 25 psi for Suc and 35 psi for auxin; the collision gas (CAD) setting was high for Suc and medium for auxin. Declustering potential and collision energy for individual multiple reaction monitoring transitions was done with further declustering potential and collision energy optimization. A specific set of multiple reaction monitoring transitions was monitored for each period according to the metabolites eluted within this period.</p></section><section id="s12"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Esculin Feeding and Confocal Microscopy</h3>
<p>We diluted esculin hydrate (Sigma-Aldrich) to 10 mM in deionized water. We performed vacuum infiltration by immersing leaves adjacent to the rose flower in esculin solution under vacuum at 0.7 MPa. We sectioned petal AZ by hand with a razor blade 10 h after esculin treatment and immediately immersed the sections in 80% (v/v) glycerol before mounting on glass slides. We recorded fluorescence using a FV1000 laser scanning confocal microscope (Olympus), with an excitation wavelength of 405 nm and emission wavelength of 454 nm.</p></section><section id="s13"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Immunolocalization of IAA</h3>
<p>We examined IAA distribution as previously described (<a href="#bib55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib55">Sakata et al., 2010</a>). We prefixed freshly prepared AZ samples for 2 h in 3% (w/v) 1-ethyl-3-(3-dimethyl-aminopropyl)-carbodiimide hydrochloride (Sigma-Aldrich) at room temperature and transferred them to 4% (w/v) paraformaldehyde (Sigma-Aldrich) and 2% (v/v) glutaraldehyde (Sigma-Aldrich). We dehydrated the samples using a gradient series of ethanol solutions (30%, 50%, 70%, 80%, 90%, 100% [v/v]). After dehydration, we transferred the samples to xylene and then paraplast (Thermo Fisher Scientific), each for 1 h, followed by embedding in 100% (v/v) paraplast. We cut the embedded samples into 10 μm sections using a microtome (HistoCore BIOCUT, Leica Biosystems). We incubated the sections with 1:100 (v/v) dilutions of anti-IAA monoclonal antibody (Sigma-Aldrich, A0855) overnight at 4°C, and then with DyLight<sup>TM</sup> 488-labeled anti-mouse IgG antibody (1:500 [v/v], KPL, 5230-0391) for 4 h at room temperature in the dark. We recorded the fluorescence signal using an SP8 laser scanning confocal microscope (Leica), with an excitation wavelength of 488 nm and emission wavelength of 518 nm. The negative controls were specimens not incubated with the anti-auxin antibody.</p></section><section id="s14"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">RNA Extraction and RT-qPCR</h3>
<p>We extracted total RNA using the hot borate method as previously described by <a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Gao et al. (2016)</a>. We synthesized first-strand cDNAs using 1 μg of total RNA with oligo d(T) and random primers in a final volume of 20 μL. For RT-qPCR, we used 1 μL cDNA as the template. We performed RT-qPCR (40 cycles of denaturation for 5 s at 95°C and annealing for 30 s at 60°C) using the Step One Plus<sup>TM</sup> real-time PCR system (Applied Biosystems) with KAPATM SYBR® FAST quantitative PCR kits (Kapa Biosystems). We used Rh<em>UBI2</em> as the reference gene (<a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Meng et al., 2013</a>). Each experiment was performed independently three times. The primers used in this study are listed in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Set 1</a>. The statistical tests are shown in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Set 2</a>.</p></section><section id="s15"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Subcellular Localization of <em>Rh</em>SUC2 and <em>Rh</em>ARF7</h3>
<p>We determined the subcellular localization of <em>Rh</em>SUC2 and <em>Rh</em>ARF7 in <em>N. benthamiana</em> leaf epidermal cells. We PCR-amplified the open reading frames (ORFs) for Rh<em>SUC2</em> and Rh<em>ARF7</em>, and inserted the PCR products at the SalI/SpeI restriction sites in the pSuper1300 vector to form SUPERpro:Rh<em>SUC2-GFP</em> and SUPERpro:Rh<em>ARF7-GFP</em>, respectively. We introduced the resulting vectors into Agrobacterium (<em>Agrobacterium tumefaciens</em>) strain GV3101. We grew Agrobacterium cultures overnight at 28°C, pelleted the cells by centrifugation at 3214<em>g</em> for 5 min at room temperature and resuspended the pellet in infiltration buffer (10 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, 10 mM MES, 200 μM acetosyringone, pH 5.6) to a final OD<sub>600</sub> = 0.8. We infiltrated Agrobacterium cell suspensions into <em>N. benthamiana</em> leaves. After 3 d, we detached infiltrated leaves and recorded fluorescence signal in tobacco epidermal cells on a laser confocal fluorescence microscopy (Olympus Fluo View FV1000). 35Spro:<em>PM-mCherry</em> (CD3-1007) and NF-YA4 (SUPERpro:<em>NF-YA4-mCherry</em>) were used as plasma membrane and nuclear markers, respectively (<a href="#bib42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib42">Nelson et al., 2007</a>; <a href="#bib75" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib75">Zhou et al., 2015</a>). The GFP fluorescence signals were observed at an excitation wavelength of 488 nm and emission wavelength of 506-538 nm, and mCherry signals were detected using excitation with a 587-nm laser and emission with a 575- to 675-nm band pass filter. The primers used in this study are listed in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Set 1</a>.</p></section><section id="s16"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">VIGS</h3>
<p>We performed VIGS-mediated gene silencing as previously described (<a href="#bib71" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib71">Wu et al., 2017</a>). Briefly, we PCR-amplified 534-bp and 414-bp fragments specific to Rh<em>SUC2</em> and Rh<em>ARF7</em> 3′ UTR regions, respectively, and inserted them separately into the pTRV2 vector, which we then introduced into Agrobacterium strain GV3101. For double silencing of Rh<em>SUC2</em> and Rh<em>ARF7</em>, we fused the 3′ UTR regions of Rh<em>SUC2</em> and Rh<em>ARF7</em> by fusion PCR, and then inserted the fused product into the pTRV2 vector. The primers used in this study are listed in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Set 1</a>.</p>
<p>We grew the relevant Agrobacterium colonies overnight in Luria-Bertani medium containing 10 mM MES (pH 6.3), 20 μM acetosyringone, 50 μg mL<sup>−1</sup> kanamycin, and 50 μg mL<sup>−1</sup> gentamycin sulfate. We collected the cells by centrifugation at 2057<em>g</em> for 10 min, and resuspended the pellet in infiltration buffer (10 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, 10 mM MES, 200 μM acetosyringone, pH 5.6) to a final OD<sub>600</sub> of 1.5. We mixed the pTRV1 and pTRV2 cultures in a 1:1 (v/v) ratio and then placed the mixtures in the dark for 3 h at room temperature.</p>
<p>We vacuum-infiltrated rose plants by immersing whole plants propagated by tissue culture for 60 d in the bacterial suspension and infiltrated under a vacuum at 0.7 MPa. We then transplanted plants into a mixture of vermiculite and nutritive soil (1:1). Before further functional analyses, we measured the expression of the target genes (Rh<em>SUC2</em> or Rh<em>ARF7</em>) by RT-qPCR in the petals at flower opening stage 2 in each plant. The plants with downregulated target gene expression were used for further assays. Three independent experiments were performed with 50 plants in each experiment. The statistical tests are shown in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Set 2</a>.</p></section><section id="s17"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Transient Expression in Rose Petal AZ</h3>
<p>We introduced the SUPERpro:Rh<em>SUC2-GFP</em> construct into Agrobacterium as above for transient overexpression of Rh<em>SUC2</em> in the flowers of Rh<em>ARF7</em>-silenced plants. We performed vacuum infiltration by immersing flowers at opening stage 1 in the bacterial suspension and infiltration under a vacuum at 0.7 MPa. Before further functional analyses, we first measured the accumulation of <em>GFP</em> transcripts in the petals at the flower opening stage 2 of inoculated plants by semi-quantitative RT-PCR. We first identified successfully inoculated plants by PCR for the presence of the GFP transgene. We then measured the expression of the target genes (Rh<em>SUC2</em> or Rh<em>ARF7</em>) in the same petals of these successfully inoculated plants by RT-qPCR. Plants with upregulated Rh<em>SUC2</em> and downregulated Rh<em>ARF7</em> expression were used for further assays.</p></section><section id="s18"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Suc Uptake Assay</h3>
<p>We cloned the ORF for Rh<em>SUC2</em> into the yeast expression vector pDR196 and transformed the resulting construct into the Suc uptake-deficient yeast strain SUSY7/<em>ura3</em> by the lithium acetate method. The pDR196 empty vector was used as the control. We spotted transformed pDR196-<em>Rh</em>SUC2 and pDR196 colonies as serial dilutions and cultured them on synthetic dropout medium (SD–Ura) with 2% (w/v) Glc or 2% (w/v) Suc as the sole carbon source at 30°C for 3 d. We recorded the growth of the various yeast strains by taking photographs.</p></section><section id="s19"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Yeast One-Hybrid Assay</h3>
<p>We inserted Rh<em>SUC2</em> promoter fragments into the pAbAi vector (Clontech) and introduced the resulting constructs into the yeast one-hybrid Y1H gold strain. The transformed cells were grown on synthetic dropout medium (SD–Ura) plus 100 ng mL<sup>−1</sup> aureobasidin A (AbA). We then introduced the recombinant vector pGADT7-Rh<em>ARF7</em> into a yeast strain harboring the pRh<em>SUC2</em> (AuxRE)-AbAi and pRh<em>SUC2</em> (mAuxRE)-AbAi. The transformed cells were grown on synthetic dropout medium (SD–Ura)_plus 200 ng mL<sup>−1</sup> AbA for 3 d. The primers used in this study are listed in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Set 1</a>.</p></section><section id="s20"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Transactivation and Dual Luciferase Reporter Assays</h3>
<p>We analyzed the transactivation of the Rh<em>SUC2</em> promoter by ARFs as previously described by <a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Kong et al. (2015)</a> and <a href="#bib68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib68">Wang et al. (2018</a>). We inserted the ORFs for the <em>ARF</em> genes into the pGreenII0029 62-SK vector (<a href="#bib17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib17">Hellens et al., 2005</a>) to generate effector constructs under control of the 35S promoter; we also cloned the Rh<em>SUC2</em> promoter into the pGreenII0800-LUC vector (<a href="#bib17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib17">Hellens et al., 2005</a>) to drive expression of the luciferase reporter. We used empty vectors as negative controls. We introduced all constructs into Agrobacterium strain GV3101 harboring the pSoup plasmid, and grew bacterial cultures overnight as described above. We collected cells by centrifugation at 3214<em>g</em> for 5 min at room temperature and resuspended the pellets in infiltration buffer (10 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, 200 μM acetosyringone, 10 mM MES, pH 5.6) to a final OD<sub>600</sub> of 0.8 before mixing cell suspensions in a 1:1 (v/v) ratio. We then infiltrated into young <em>N. benthamiana</em> leaves. After 3 d, we harvested the infiltrated leaves and sprayed them with 50 mg L<sup>−1</sup> D-luciferin (Promega). We captured images of LUC signals with a charge-coupled device camera (CHEMIPROHT 1300B/LND, 16 bits, Roper Scientific) at –110°C.</p>
<p>For dual luciferase reporter assays, we coinfiltrated different bacterial mixtures described above with Agrobacterium cells harboring a 35Spro:REN construct [where the <em>Renilla luciferase</em> gene (<em>REN</em>) is under the control of the 35S promoter] into <em>N. benthamiana</em> leaves as previously described by <a href="#bib69" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib69">Wei et al. (2017)</a>. After 3 d, we measured LUC and REN activities with the dual-luciferase reporter assay reagents (Promega) and a GloMax 20/20 luminometer (Promega). The primers used in this study are listed in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Set 1</a>. The statistical tests are shown in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Set 2</a>.</p></section><section id="s21"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">EMSA</h3>
<p>We performed EMSA assays according to the instructions of the Light Shift chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). We cloned the Rh<em>ARF7</em> ORF into the GST vector pGEX-4T-2 (GE Healthcare) and transformed the resulting construct into E. <em>coli</em> Rosetta cells. We induced the production of the GST-<em>Rh</em>ARF7 fusion protein by the addition of 0.6 mM isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside in 100 mL in Luria-Bertani medium at 16°C for 12 h. We extracted and purified the fusion protein with glutathione Sepharose 4B beads (GE Healthcare) following the manufacturer’s instructions. The biotin-labeled probes were designed as described in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Set 1</a>.</p></section><section id="s22"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Sequence Analysis</h3>
<p>We aligned amino acid sequences with default parameters using ClustalW (<a href="https://www.genome.jp/tools-bin/clustalw" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://www.genome.jp/tools-bin/clustalw</a>). We constructed the phylogenetic tree based on the alignment result using the neighbor-joining method with MEGA version 5.05 (<a href="#bib62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib62">Tamura et al., 2011</a>) with the following parameters: 1000 bootstrap replicates, Poisson correction, partial deletion, and uniform rates. The protein transmembrane prediction was performed on the TMHMM server (<a href="http://www.cb-s.dtu.dk/services/TMHMM-2.0/" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://www.cb-s.dtu.dk/services/TMHMM-2.0/</a>; <a href="#bib39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib39">Möller et al., 2001</a>). The files of phylogenetic analysis and protein alignment are shown in <a href="http://www.plantcell.org/cgi/content/full/tpc.19.00695/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Data Sets 3 and 4</a>.</p></section><section id="s23"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Accession Numbers</h3>
